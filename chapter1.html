Chapter One

The Breaking Point

Some storms do not begin with violence. Some begin with words, promises, and the slow breaking of trust.

Justin once believed that love meant loyalty. When someone said, “I love you,” he thought it meant safety, trust, and peace. But with Sam, love became a storm. It began softly enough with late-night talks, laughter, and promises of forever. Sam could be charming when he wanted to be. He knew how to make Justin feel seen and wanted. Yet over time, that warmth twisted into something colder, leaving bruises both visible and hidden.

Sam had a drinking problem. Justin told himself it was stressful, that work had been too much, that everyone had bad nights. But bad nights turned into bad weeks, and bad weeks became their normal life. When Sam drank, the sweetness disappeared. His eyes grew dark and distant. He accused Justin of things that never happened, yelled until his voice broke, and sometimes shoved him hard enough to leave marks.

Then there were the nights Sam never came home. Justin knew where he was, though he never said it out loud. The perfume on Sam’s clothes told him everything. So did the texts that appeared on Sam’s phone when he forgot to lock it. Sam always had a new excuse, a new story, a new apology. And every time, Justin wanted to believe it.

“You’re the only one who really gets me,” Sam would slur after a night out, wrapping his arms around Justin. “I mess up, but I love you. You know that, right?”

Justin would nod, not because he believed it, but because he was too tired to argue. He convinced himself that maybe this was what love looked like for people who were broken.

The night everything shattered was cold and rainy. Sam stumbled around two in the morning, reeking of alcohol. His words were sharp and his eyes wild. He accused Justin of lying, of not trusting him, of making him this way. Justin tried to calm him, tried to walk away, but Sam grabbed his arm and shoved him into the wall. The world spun. The last thing Justin remembered was the sound of glass breaking and his head hitting the floor.

He woke up hours later on the bathroom tile, shaking and bleeding. For a long time, he didn’t move. He just stared at the ceiling and whispered, “This can’t be love. This can’t be love.”

Trisha found him the next morning. She was the only friend he hadn’t pushed away. When he didn’t answer her calls, she came by with coffee and saw the state he was in. Her face went pale.

“Justin,” she said softly, crouching beside him. “What did he do?”

Justin couldn’t answer. He just cried.

Trisha helped him pack a small bag, her hands trembling as she folded his clothes. “You’re not going back,” she said. “You’re coming with me.”

He stayed on her couch for weeks, jumpy and quiet, waking from nightmares that left him gasping for air. Every time his phone buzzed, he froze, expecting it to be Sam. Sometimes it was. The messages swung between apologies and threats. I miss you. Then, you’ll never find someone like me.

Trisha finally took his phone one night and turned it off. “You don’t owe him another chance,” she said. “You owe yourself peace.”

It wasn’t easy. Justin missed the version of Sam that once made him laugh. He missed the feeling of being loved, even though he knew now it wasn’t real love at all. Healing felt harder than staying ever had. But Trisha didn’t let him give up.

Little by little, Justin started living again. He went to therapy. He began walking in the mornings, breathing in the quiet. He even started painting again, something he hadn’t done since before Sam. The first thing he painted was a window with light breaking through storm clouds.

One evening, Trisha found him sitting by the window, sunlight spilling over him. “You’re smiling,” she said. “That’s new.”

Justin looked at her and nodded. “I’m not all the way better,” he said. “But I think I’m finally learning what love isn’t.”

Trisha smiled. “That’s the best place to start.”

For the first time in a long time, Justin believed her. The past was still there, but it no longer owned him. The fear, the pain, the lies, they were all still memories. But that was all they were now. Just memories.

He was done surviving. He was ready to live.
