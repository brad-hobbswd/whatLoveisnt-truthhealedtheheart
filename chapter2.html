Chapter Two

The Familiar Stranger

Healing often begins quietly, through moments that seem ordinary before life reveals their deeper meaning.

It had been nearly six months since Justin left Sam. The nightmares still came, but they no longer controlled his nights. Trisha checked on him every morning before work. She tried not to mother him, but she could not help it. He had been broken before, but never like this.

One Sunday, after another long week of silence and sleeplessness, Trisha knocked on his door holding two cups of coffee.

“Come with me,” she said.

“Where?” Justin asked.

“Church.”

He almost laughed.

“You? Church?”

She grinned.

“Don’t look so shocked. I wasn’t always this backslidden, remember? I used to drag you to Sunday school when we were kids.”

“Yeah, and we both fell asleep during the lesson,” he said, smiling faintly.

Trisha shrugged.

“Maybe this time we will stay awake. You need peace, Justin. You keep saying you want to heal. Maybe it starts here.”

He hesitated, but something in her tone made him agree. He was not sure he was searching for God. He did not even know where to begin with that. But he was searching for something, anything that did not remind him of Sam.

The church was small but full of life. The music was gentle and heartfelt, and the air carried a quiet sense of safety. Justin sat near the back, hoping to blend in. Trisha sang softly beside him, her voice steady and calm.

As he looked across the congregation, his eyes landed on a man near the front row. He was tall, with kind eyes and a peaceful smile. There was something familiar about him, though Justin could not place it. Maybe from school. Maybe from another time. The man turned slightly, and their eyes met. It lasted only a moment, but it was enough to stir something warm inside Justin, something he had not felt in a long while.

After the service, Trisha convinced him to stay for coffee in the fellowship hall. People greeted her with hugs and laughter, surprised to see her back. Justin lingered by the snack table, unsure of what to say or where to stand. That was when the man from the front row approached him.

“Hey,” the man said with a smile. “I’m Brad.”

Justin turned, startled. “Oh, hey. I’m Justin.”

“You’re new here,” Brad said in an easy, friendly tone. “Or just visiting?”

“Visiting,” Justin replied. “First time in years.”

Brad nodded. “I get that. I left for a while too. Sometimes you have to step away before you can come home.”

Justin looked at him more closely. There was no judgment in his voice, only understanding.

“I swear I know you from somewhere,” Justin said. “Did you go to Knox Central High?”

Brad’s eyes brightened. “Yeah. Class of 2005.”

Justin smiled. “No way. I was class of 2006.”

“That explains it,” Brad said. “You used to hang out with Trisha, right? You two were always together.”

Justin nodded. “Still are.”

Brad laughed. “Good to see that some things stay the same.”

Trisha walked over, balancing two donuts.

“So, you two finally met. Brad, this is my best friend, Justin. Justin, this is Brad. He used to play guitar in the youth group band before he became a responsible adult.”

Brad chuckled. “Responsible might be pushing it.”

Justin smiled. It had been a long time since someone made him laugh that easily.

They talked for a while. The conversation stayed light, circling around music, old teachers, and small town stories. Still, something in Justin began to settle. Brad was different. He listened. He did not pry, did not rush to fill the silence, and did not treat him like someone who needed fixing.

When they left, Trisha nudged him as they walked to the car.

“You’re smiling again,” she said.

“Am I?” Justin asked, almost embarrassed.

“Yes,” she said softly. “That’s new.”

He glanced back toward the church. Brad stood on the steps, waving goodbye to someone. Their eyes met again, and Brad gave a small nod.

Justin looked away quickly, uncertain what to do with the feeling. He was not ready for anyone new. He did not even want to think about it. But something about Brad felt steady and kind.

He did not know it then, but that small encounter would be the first of many. Healing often began in unexpected places, and sometimes it started with a familiar stranger who could see the good in you, even when you could not see it yourself.
