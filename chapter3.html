Chapter Three

Learning to Trust Again

Healing rarely arrives all at once. Sometimes trust returns quietly through people who remain gentle with wounded hearts.

The weeks that followed settled into a rhythm Justin had not expected. Trisha continued to bring him to church, insisting that even if he did not believe, being around people who cared could only help. Justin protested in silence, but curiosity kept him going.

Brad was always there. Sometimes he played guitar with the youth group. Other times he helped set up chairs or handed out programs. He smiled at Justin as if it were nothing, but Justin noticed. Each time their eyes met, he felt a flicker of warmth that both comforted and unsettled him.

At first, Justin kept his distance. He was afraid of what it meant to open up, even in small ways. Sam’s memory lingered, and every time Justin imagined growing close to someone new, he feared it would end the same way, with trust broken and another part of him bruised.

One evening, Trisha insisted they stop for coffee after service. Justin tried to refuse, but Brad was already sitting at a table, his guitar case resting beside him.

“Hey,” Brad said, smiling as Justin approached. “I’ve been meaning to ask. Do you play?”

Justin shook his head. “Not really. I used to, a long time ago.”

“That’s all right,” Brad said. “We all have our pasts.”

His tone was gentle and without pressure, and that small moment of understanding made Justin feel safer than he had in months.

For a while, they sat quietly. Then Brad spoke again:

“I don’t know your story, and I’m not going to ask for it. But if you ever need someone to sit with, I’ll do that. No judgment. No expectations.”

Justin swallowed hard. That was new. He was used to people pushing, prying, or trying to fix him before he was ready. Brad offered something different. Presence. A kind of quiet steadiness Justin had not realized he needed.

Over the next few weeks, their friendship grew naturally. They did not rush into deep conversations. Sometimes they sat together during the service. Other times they walked around the church courtyard, laughing over childhood memories and the awkward moments they shared with Trisha growing up.

Justin began to notice changes within himself. He laughed more, though carefully. He no longer flinched when someone brushed his arm. He started to breathe again without feeling the weight of Sam’s shadow pressing on him.

There were still nights when the memories came. He would wake drenched in sweat, hearing Sam’s voice or seeing his face. But now, instead of letting the panic overtake him, he would reach for his phone, see a message from Trisha, or remember Brad’s quiet strength. The fear still came, but it no longer swallowed him whole.

One afternoon, while they were helping set up chairs for the youth group, Justin hesitated before lifting a heavy stack. Brad noticed immediately.

“You okay?” he asked.

Justin paused, the weight of both the chairs and his thoughts heavy in his hands. “Yeah,” he said finally. “Just thinking.”

Brad nodded and stayed nearby without saying another word. That simple act, staying close without needing to fix anything, felt like something sacred. It reminded Justin that not everyone would hurt him, that some people were safe to stand beside.

It was not love, at least not yet. It was trust. Trust that he could exist near someone without fear. Trust that someone could see him as more than his pain. Trust that healing could begin without being shattered again.

For Justin, that was everything.
